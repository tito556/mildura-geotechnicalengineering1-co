Raft Foundation Design in Mildura: Engineered Solutions for Reactive Soils

Together, we solve the challenges of tomorrow.

LEARN MORE →

With over 35,000 residents spread across the Murray River floodplain, Mildura presents a unique geotechnical landscape where raft foundation design has become the standard for medium to heavy structures. The Quaternary alluvial deposits that underlie much of the city, particularly the Blanchetown Clay and Coonambidgal Formation, exhibit high reactivity with seasonal moisture fluctuations exceeding 40 mm of surface movement in extreme years. A properly engineered raft or mat foundation distributes structural loads across a wide footprint, mitigating differential settlement that would crack conventional strip footings within the first decade. Our geotechnical team combines borehole logging per AS 1726 with laboratory shrink-swell testing to feed accurate soil parameters into the finite element models. Before committing to a raft design, many projects in the Mildura region benefit from a complementary site investigation with SPT boreholes to establish refusal depth and bearing strata, particularly where paleochannels of the ancient Murray River create hidden soft zones beneath otherwise competent surface clays. The interaction between slab stiffness, beam depth, and soil reactivity class dictates every reinforcement schedule we produce, and Mildura's H2-D classification under AS 2870 means no two sites can be treated with a generic approach.

A raft foundation designed for Mildura's H2-D reactive clays must manage both the 40 mm of seasonal heave and the long-term consolidation of alluvial paleochannel deposits beneath the Murray Basin floodplain.

Our approach and scope

A common mistake we see in Mildura is the assumption that a thicker slab automatically compensates for poor site preparation. Pouring a 150 mm raft over uncontrolled fill with no edge beam deepening into natural ground generates a rigid plate that simply tilts as a unit when the underlying material consolidates unevenly. The correct methodology starts with classifying the site according to AS 2870-2011, determining the characteristic surface movement (ys), and then selecting a beam grid spacing — typically 4 to 6 metres for Class H2 sites in the Mildura region — that limits differential deflection to less than L/300. Reinforcement detailing must account for both sagging and hogging moments induced by edge moisture variation, a phenomenon well-documented in semi-arid climates like Sunraysia where irrigation adjacent to slabs creates localized swelling. For commercial structures exceeding two storeys, we often recommend supplementing the raft analysis with deep foundation assessment via pile load testing when the combined dead and live loads approach 150 kPa and the underlying clay has an undrained shear strength below 75 kPa. The design also integrates vapour barriers and underslab drainage layers, critical in Mildura's clay soils where capillary rise can saturate the subgrade within months of construction if not intercepted.

Site-specific factors

Mildura's semi-arid climate, with average annual rainfall below 300 mm punctuated by intense summer storm events exceeding 50 mm in a single day, creates extreme wetting-drying cycles in the near-surface clay profile. A raft foundation designed only for the dry condition risks edge heave when landscape irrigation or broken stormwater pipes introduce moisture to the perimeter, while a design that ignores the desiccated summer crust may underestimate the stiffness contrast between the upper metre and the deeper clay. The Murray Basin's stratigraphy adds another layer of complexity: lenses of Parilla Sand interbedded with the Blanchetown Clay can act as preferential drainage paths, concentrating moisture beneath one quadrant of a raft and inducing tilt. Differential movements exceeding 15 mm between column lines in a commercial building will manifest as inoperable door frames, cracked partition walls, and stressed mechanical connections within two to three seasonal cycles. The design must also address the potential for shrink-swell in the zone of influence, which extends to a depth of approximately 2.5 to 3.0 metres in Mildura's soil moisture regime. Ignoring this depth when specifying beam penetration or underslab preparation means the foundation sits within the active zone rather than bridging it.

Quick answers

What is the typical cost range for a raft foundation design in Mildura?

For a standard residential waffle raft on a Class H2 site in Mildura, the engineering design and documentation typically ranges from AU$1,450 to AU$3,200 depending on the complexity of the floor plan and number of articulation joints required. Larger commercial mat foundations with finite element modelling and integrated pile design fall between AU$4,200 and AU$7,050. These figures cover site classification, structural design, reinforcement detailing, and the signed Form 15 compliance certificate.

How does Mildura's soil reactivity affect slab reinforcement requirements?

Mildura's Blanchetown Clay exhibits high liquid limits, typically 50-70%, placing most sites in Class H2 under AS 2870 with characteristic surface movement (ys) values between 40 and 60 mm. This necessitates deeper edge beams (minimum 400 mm into natural ground), closer beam grid spacing, and heavier reinforcement than a Class M or H1 site. The reinforcement must resist both the sagging moment under internal walls during dry periods and the hogging moment at the perimeter during wet periods, often requiring top and bottom steel in edge beams rather than the single-layer mesh sufficient for less reactive sites.

What geotechnical investigations are required before designing a raft foundation in Mildura?

A minimum of two boreholes to 3.0 metres depth per AS 1726 is standard, with Atterberg limits, linear shrinkage, and soil suction profiles measured at 0.5 metre intervals through the active zone. If laboratory shrink-swell testing returns a reactivity classification of H2 or higher, the design proceeds under AS 2870 Section 3 for stiffened rafts. Where the borehole logs encounter sand lenses or paleochannel deposits within the zone of influence, additional investigation via cone penetration testing may be warranted to map the extent of the anomaly before finalizing beam depths and grid layout.
